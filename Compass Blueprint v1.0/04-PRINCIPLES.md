# Compass Principles

> *"Principles translate philosophy into everyday decisions."*

---

# Purpose

The Compass Principles define how we apply our philosophy when designing, building, and evolving the product.

Philosophy explains what we believe.

Principles explain how those beliefs influence our daily decisions.

Every feature, interface, data model, and engineering decision should reflect these principles.

---

# Principle 1 — Patient First

Every decision should ultimately benefit the patient.

Features should improve understanding, communication, or clinical decision-making.

Technology is never the goal.

Better patient care is.

---

# Principle 2 — Journey First

Compass is organized around the patient's clinical journey.

Every observation, consultation, diagnosis, medication, investigation, and outcome exists as part of a continuous story.

The journey is the primary organizing concept of Compass.

---

# Principle 3 — Evidence is Sacred

Every important statement should be traceable to evidence.

Evidence may include:

- Clinical notes
- Laboratory results
- Imaging studies
- Prescriptions
- Reports
- Observations

Users should always understand where information comes from.

---

# Principle 4 — Preserve Context

Medical information should never be separated from its context.

Whenever possible, Compass should answer not only:

> What happened?

but also:

> Why?

> When?

> What changed?

> What happened next?

Context transforms information into understanding.

---

# Principle 5 — Model Reality

The domain model should reflect clinical reality—not software convenience.

If clinicians think in terms of Clinical Journeys, Care Teams, Evidence, and Follow-up Plans, Compass should use the same language.

The software should adapt to medicine.

Medicine should not adapt to the software.

---

# Principle 6 — Design for Understanding

Interfaces should reduce cognitive effort.

Users should spend their attention understanding the patient—not understanding the software.

When multiple designs are possible, choose the one that communicates more clearly.

---

# Principle 7 — Speak the Domain Language

Use the language clinicians and caregivers naturally use.

Prefer:

- Clinical Journey
- Care Team
- Imaging Study
- Laboratory Result

Avoid technical or ambiguous terminology when domain language is available.

The vocabulary of Compass is part of its user experience.

---

# Principle 8 — Design Before Build

Every significant capability should be designed before it is implemented.

The Blueprint exists to explore ideas while they are inexpensive to change.

Code should be the final expression of a decision—not the place where the decision is made.

---

# Principle 9 — Build for Evolution

Healthcare evolves.

Technology evolves.

Compass should be designed to evolve without losing its identity.

Flexibility should come from thoughtful design—not unnecessary complexity.

---

# Principle 10 — Trust is Earned

Trust is one of Compass's most valuable assets.

Users should understand:

- What they are seeing.
- Where the information came from.
- Why the system reached a conclusion.

Trust grows through transparency, consistency, and evidence.

---

# Putting the Principles into Practice

Before introducing a feature, ask:

1. Does it improve the patient's journey?
2. Does it strengthen evidence?
3. Does it preserve context?
4. Does it reduce cognitive effort?
5. Does it use the language of medicine?
6. Will it still make sense five years from now?

If the answer to several of these questions is "no", the feature should be reconsidered.

# Founder Notes

## Why does this document exist?

To translate Compass's philosophy into practical guidance for product, design, engineering, and future contributors.

## What future decisions should it guide?

Feature design, domain modeling, architecture, user experience, engineering, documentation, and AI integration.

## What must never change?

Compass should always prioritize patients, evidence, context, clarity, and trust over technical convenience.
